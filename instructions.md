# Ctrl Alt Defeat
### NCF's Responsible Cyber Operations Game

In the digital age, cyberspace has become a battleground where global threats are constantly evolving. The UK's ability to navigate and defend this space is crucial to achieving its national objectives. However, our adversaries – from rogue states to criminal hackers – are using the same technologies to challenge and disrupt our security. The UK cannot leave cyberspace an uncontested realm where adversaries operate with impunity – this is where you come in.

In Ctrl Alt Defeat, your mission is to conduct responsible cyber operations to exploit the adversary's reliance on digital technology and secure the UK's digital borders.

The challenge awaits. Are you ready to play?

---

## How to Play

### Aim of the Game

Achieve your mission objective by conducting cyber operations using Intelligence, Technology, Governance, and Cyber Intervention Cards, making sure to balance effectiveness and responsibility. The winner is the first player to reach the centre of the board. The more responsible your cyber operations are, the quicker you will advance around the board.

### Set Up

1. Remove the blank Objective and Action Cards if you are not using them.
2. Shuffle the Objective and Action Cards but keep the decks separate.
3. Deal each player two Objective Cards and four Action Cards.
4. Place the Objective and Action Card piles next to the board.
5. Roll a die to decide who goes first – highest wins.
6. Play rotates clockwise around the board.

---

## Cards

### Objective Cards

To move around the board, players must conduct cyber operations by fulfilling the tasks outlined on the Objective Cards. Players may hold a maximum of two Objective Cards.

### Action Cards

Action Cards are made up of Intelligence, Technology, Governance and Cyber Interventions – the elements needed to conduct a cyber operation and complete the mission on your Objective Card. Players may hold a maximum of six Action Cards at the end of each turn.

### Glitch Cards

Glitch Cards can have a positive or negative effect on the player's game. If a player draws a Glitch Card, it must be played immediately at the start of their turn. If, while playing a Glitch Card, further Glitch Cards are drawn, they must be played until no Glitch Cards remain in the hand.

---

## On Your Turn

### Building Your Operation

1. Draw two Action Cards from the deck or take the top card from the Cache if it is not a Glitch Card. If the Action Card pile runs out, shuffle the Cache and place the cards face down to form a new Action Card pile.
2. Immediately play any Glitch Cards in your hand until you have no Glitch Cards remaining. Place any played or discarded cards face up on the Cache.
3. Attempt to build your cyber operation by combining an Objective Card with one of each type of Action Card (Intelligence, Technology, Governance and Cyber Intervention). You must have one of each type of card to complete an operation.
4. If you can build a complete operation, check that you are happy with the responsibility and effectiveness score before choosing to play it. You may pass your turn if you cannot, or do not wish to, play an operation.
5. Discard your choice of Action Cards face up on the Cache to keep a maximum of six in your hand.

### Playing an Operation

1. If you are ready to conduct your operation, place the cards on the corresponding locations on the board. Describe the operation and explain your choices to the other players.
2. Determine the operation's success and score by following the steps in the scoring section below.
3. If playing to win the game: If your successful operation would take you over the finishing line, it must have a responsibility score of 3 or more.
4. If your operation succeeds, move your counter forwards by the operation's effectiveness score. If your operation's responsibility score was 4 or higher, move your counter forwards an extra place and draw two Action Cards.
5. If your operation fails, your counter remains in its current position.
6. Place any used Action Cards face up on the Cache, and the Objective Card at the bottom of the Objective Cards' deck.
7. Draw a new Objective Card to keep two in your hand.

---

## Scoring

Once you are ready to conduct an operation, determine its outcome by:

1. Adding up the responsibility scores of all the cards in your operation, including the Objective Card.
2. If the responsibility score is 4 or higher, your operation succeeds.
3. If the responsibility score is 1, 2, or 3, roll a die to determine success:

| Die Roll | Result  |
|----------|---------|
| 1–2      | Fail    |
| 3–6      | Success |

4. If the responsibility score is 0 or less, roll a die to determine success:

| Die Roll | Result                                  |
|----------|-----------------------------------------|
| 1–2      | Player goes offline and must miss a turn |
| 3–5      | Fail                                    |
| 6        | Success                                 |

5. If your operation fails, it scores 0 for effectiveness.
6. If your operation succeeds, add up the effectiveness scores of all the cards in your operation, including the Objective Card, to give the operation's total effectiveness score.

## Advanced Adversary – The Coalition of Allies

The advanced rules allow for more realism, teamwork and competition. Players will have to think on their feet, strategise, and work with each other.

### Aim of the Game

The base rules remain unchanged, but these additional rules apply:

### Personalities

Players will now be able to compete as different personalities:

- One player (or the facilitator) will be the Adversary.
- All other players will form the Coalition of Allies.

### Offensive Operations

A player may target any other player when conducting an operation. Responsibility is calculated as usual to determine success or failure. If successful, the target moves their counter backwards by the effectiveness score on the operation's Objective Card.

### Winning

The winner is the first player to reach the centre of the board. The Allies may wish to win individually, but also to collaborate to stop the Adversary. A final Ally operation to get to the centre of the board must have a responsibility score of 3 or more.

---

## The Adversary

The Adversary is an irresponsible cyber actor who seeks to use cyber capabilities irresponsibly to cause harm. The following rules apply to them:

1. The Adversary ignores responsibility scores. Instead, success is determined by a die roll:

| Die Roll | Result                                                        |
|----------|---------------------------------------------------------------|
| 1        | Go offline for one turn, or discard two Action Cards          |
| 2–4      | Partial success: score half of your operation's effectiveness (rounded up to the nearest whole number) |
| 5–6      | Complete success: score your full operation's effectiveness   |

2. The Adversary operation must always include a Cyber Intervention and a Technology Card, but Governance and Intelligence Cards are optional.

---

## The Coalition of Allies

Allies follow the base rules and may also collaborate in these ways:

1. They may show cards to each other (partial or full hands).
2. They may ask for one Action Card (but not a Governance Card) from an Ally to complete an operation.
3. They may swap one Action Card (but not a Governance Card) per turn with an Ally.

---

*This game supports understanding how the UK government conducts cyber operations responsibly, in contrast to our adversaries, and highlights the oversight mechanisms in place to ensure accountability and precision. While the game includes actions that could be carried out by nation-states, this does not imply that they are, or should be, carried out without consideration. Responsibility and effectiveness scores are an illustration only to serve gameplay and should not be read as official UK policy. Responsibility and accountability are at the heart of every cyber operation, and this is reflected in the overall objective of the game.*
